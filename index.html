What is Travel Insurance?

Travel Insurance Explained: Coverage, Costs & Requirements for Indian Travellers

Travel insurance is a specialised insurance policy that protects travellers against financial losses and medical emergencies that may occur before or during a trip. Regulated by the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI), travel insurance policies sold in India must comply with standardised guidelines covering policy wordings, claim settlement procedures, and policyholder protection mechanisms.

A standard travel insurance policy for Indian travellers typically includes:

  • Medical emergencies and hospitalisation — including doctor visits, prescription medications, surgery, and emergency medical evacuation to the nearest adequate facility or repatriation to India
  • Trip cancellation and interruption — reimbursement of non-refundable trip costs if your journey is cancelled or cut short due to covered reasons such as illness, natural disaster, or visa denial
  • Baggage loss, delay, or damage — compensation for lost, stolen, or delayed luggage and personal belongings, with delay coverage typically activating after 6 to 12 hours
  • Flight delays and missed connections — coverage for meals, accommodation, and alternate transport during prolonged delays, subject to waiting periods defined in the policy
  • Personal accident and liability — lump-sum compensation for accidental death or permanent disability, plus legal defence costs if you accidentally injure someone or damage property abroad

With over 27 million Indian travellers going abroad annually (pre-pandemic peak) and more than 40 countries mandating travel insurance for visa approval, understanding how travel insurance works is essential for every Indian traveller — whether you are a student, tourist, business traveller, or senior citizen planning your next trip.

Why Travel Insurance?

Why Every Indian Traveller Should Understand Travel Insurance

Travel insurance protects you against financial losses and medical emergencies while travelling. For Indian travellers going abroad, it is often a visa requirement and always a wise financial precaution.

🏥

Medical Emergencies

Healthcare abroad can cost thousands of rupees per day. Travel insurance covers hospitalisation, doctor visits, prescriptions, and emergency evacuation. Schengen visas require a minimum of €30,000 in medical coverage — we explain what to look for in a policy.

📦

Baggage & Trip Protection

Coverage for lost, delayed, or damaged baggage, plus trip cancellation or interruption due to illness, natural disasters, or airline insolvency. Know what compensation you are entitled to under standard Indian travel insurance policies.

📜

Visa Compliance

Over 40 countries require travel insurance as a visa condition — Schengen (€30,000 minimum cover), USA, Australia, Canada, UAE, and many more. We break down the requirements so you know exactly what your policy needs to include.

Personal Liability

If you accidentally cause injury or damage property abroad, legal costs can be substantial. Many policies include personal liability coverage of up to ₹50 lakh — understand what is covered and what is excluded.

⚠

Flight Delays & Missed Connections

Prolonged delays can mean unexpected meals, accommodation, and rebooking costs. Most policies cover these expenses, and some also cover missed connecting flights. Know the waiting periods and claim limits.

Round-the-clock support including medical coordination, translator services, travel assistance, and emergency cash advances. Save your insurer's 24/7 helpline before departure — it makes all the difference in a crisis.

Who Needs It

Who Should Buy Travel Insurance?

Travel insurance benefits different travellers in different ways. Here is who needs it most and what to look for in a policy.

🎓

Students Studying Abroad

International students need comprehensive medical coverage for the duration of their course. Many universities mandate health insurance, and travel insurance bridges coverage gaps during travel to and from India, semester breaks, and internships abroad. Look for policies with long-term coverage options and adequate medical limits for the host country's healthcare costs.

👪

Family Travellers

Families travelling together face multiplied risks — medical emergencies affecting any member, lost baggage with essential children's items, and trip cancellations affecting multiple tickets. Family floater policies offer cost-effective coverage for the entire family under a single plan at significantly lower total premiums than individual policies.

👴

Senior Citizens

Travellers over 60 face higher health risks and correspondingly higher premiums. Several Indian insurers offer specialised senior citizen travel insurance with pre-existing condition coverage (subject to disclosure and underwriting), higher medical limits, and tailored emergency assistance for age-related concerns. Compare policies designed specifically for senior travellers rather than standard plans.

💼

Business Travellers

Frequent business travellers benefit most from annual multi-trip policies covering unlimited trips within a year under a single premium. Coverage typically includes lost business equipment, trip disruptions affecting work commitments, and medical emergencies — usually at lower total cost than buying separate per-trip policies throughout the year.

⛰

Adventure Travellers

Travellers planning adventure sports — trekking, scuba diving, skiing, bungee jumping, or parasailing — need specialised coverage because standard travel insurance policies exclude high-risk activities. Adventure add-ons cover medical costs from activity-related injuries and emergency rescue or evacuation from remote locations.

✈

Visa Applicants

Anyone applying for a visa to a country that mandates travel insurance must purchase a compliant policy before visa approval. This includes all 27 Schengen states (€30,000 minimum medical cover), USA (J-1 visa holders), Australia, Canada, UAE, Japan, and several others. The policy must remain valid for the entire intended stay duration.

IRDAI Compliance

IRDAI Regulations & Policyholder Protections for Travel Insurance

Travel insurance in India is governed by the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI), ensuring standardised coverage, fair claim practices, and robust policyholder protections.

Standardised Policy Wording

IRDAI mandates that all travel insurance policies sold in India use standardised policy wordings for key coverage sections. This regulatory requirement ensures that core policy terms, definitions, coverage descriptions, and exclusions are consistent across insurers — making it easier for policyholders to compare policies from different providers on an apples-to-apples basis.

Claim Settlement Timeframe

Under the IRDAI (Protection of Policyholders' Interests) Regulations, 2017, insurers must acknowledge receipt of a claim within 14 days and settle or reject it within 30 days of receiving all required documents. For overseas medical emergencies, insurers are required to facilitate cashless treatment through their international network of healthcare providers wherever possible, minimising out-of-pocket expenses for policyholders.

Free Look Period

Policyholders receive a 15-day free look period from the date of policy receipt to review the terms and conditions. If the policy is returned within this period, the insurer must refund the premium after deducting the proportionate risk premium for the coverage period and applicable stamp duty charges. This right primarily applies to long-term or multi-year travel insurance policies rather than single-trip policies purchased close to departure.

Grievance Redressal Mechanism

If your claim is rejected or you have a complaint about your travel insurance policy, IRDAI provides a structured three-tier grievance redressal process:

Level 1 — Insurer's Internal Grievance Cell: Lodge your complaint directly with the insurer's customer service or grievance redressal department. Insurers are required to resolve complaints within 14 days of receipt.

Level 2 — IRDAI IGMS Portal: If the insurer's response is unsatisfactory, escalate your grievance through the IRDAI Integrated Grievance Management System (IGMS). This centralised portal allows policyholders to file and track complaints online.

Level 3 — Insurance Ombudsman: If the grievance remains unresolved after 30 days from IGMS filing, approach the Insurance Ombudsman for binding arbitration. The ombudsman's decision is binding on the insurer but not on the policyholder, who retains the right to approach consumer courts or other legal forums.

All information on this page is based on publicly available IRDAI circulars, guidelines, and regulations. For the latest updates and official notifications, visit the official IRDAI website.

How to Choose

How to Choose the Right Travel Insurance Policy

Follow this step-by-step guide to select a travel insurance policy that matches your specific needs, destination requirements, and budget.

  1. Assess your destination's visa and coverage requirements — Check whether your destination country mandates travel insurance and the minimum coverage required. Schengen nations require a minimum of €30,000 in medical coverage. Some countries (USA, Australia, UAE) specify minimum coverage amounts for visa applicants. Others may mandate specific coverage types such as repatriation of remains.
  2. Evaluate your personal risk profile and trip type — Consider your age, existing health conditions, trip duration, destinations, and planned activities. Senior citizens, travellers with pre-existing medical conditions, and those planning adventure sports need specialised coverage that standard off-the-shelf policies may not provide. Longer trips and multi-country itineraries require higher coverage limits.
  3. Compare coverage limits, sub-limits, and exclusions — Never choose a policy based solely on premium amount. Compare the sum insured for each coverage category, per-incident deductibles (excess), sub-limits on specific items (e.g., laptop, camera equipment), and the complete list of exclusions. A lower premium almost always means lower coverage limits, higher deductibles, or more exclusions.
  4. Research the insurer's claim settlement record — Check the insurer's claim settlement ratio and average claim settlement time. IRDAI publishes annual reports with insurer-wise claim settlement statistics. Read policyholder reviews on independent platforms. An insurer with a high claim settlement ratio and positive reviews is more likely to process your claim smoothly when needed.
  5. Read the policy wording in full before purchasing — The policy wording is a legal document that defines exactly what is and is not covered. Pay special attention to the general exclusions section — common exclusions include pre-existing medical conditions (unless specifically covered), high-risk adventure activities, travel to sanctioned or war-torn countries, and claims arising from alcohol or drug use. Understanding exclusions is as important as understanding coverage.
  6. Buy your policy as early as possible — Purchase travel insurance immediately after booking your trip, ideally at least 7 to 15 days before departure. Early purchase ensures coverage for pre-departure cancellations, medical emergencies that occur before your trip begins, and any unforeseen events — such as a natural disaster at your destination — that may force you to cancel or change your plans.

Safety First

Travel Safety Tips for Indian Travellers

Essential tips to ensure a safe and worry-free journey abroad.

  • Research your destination — understand local laws, customs, healthcare facilities, and emergency contact numbers before you travel.
  • Keep digital and physical copies of your passport, visa, insurance certificate, travel tickets, and emergency contacts stored separately.
  • Register with the Indian embassy or consulate via the MADAD portal (madad.gov.in) for assistance during emergencies.
  • Share your itinerary with family or friends back home, including flight details, accommodation addresses, and contact numbers.
  • Carry emergency numbers — your insurer's 24/7 assistance hotline, local emergency services, and Indian embassy contact information.
  • Read your policy before you travel — understand coverage limits, exclusions, and claim procedures. Don't wait until an emergency to find out what your policy covers.
  • Check COVID-19 coverage — confirm whether your policy covers pandemic-related medical expenses, quarantine costs, and trip cancellations due to COVID-19.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions About Travel Insurance

Common questions Indian travellers ask about travel insurance, answered clearly and factually.

Travel insurance is mandatory for visa applications to Schengen area countries, the USA, Australia, Canada, UAE, and several other nations. Even when not mandatory, it is strongly recommended for financial protection against medical emergencies, trip cancellations, and travel disruptions abroad. Medical costs in countries like the USA can easily exceed ₹50 lakh without insurance.

Standard coverage includes medical emergencies and hospitalisation, trip cancellation and interruption, baggage loss or delay, flight delays, personal accidents, personal liability, and emergency medical evacuation. Specific coverage limits, sub-limits, deductibles, and exclusions vary by policy and insurer. Always read the policy wording for complete details.

Premiums vary based on destination, traveller age, trip duration, and sum insured. A typical 15-day trip to Europe for a 30-year-old may cost between INR 500 to INR 2,000. A trip to the USA for the same duration may cost INR 1,000 to INR 5,000 due to higher medical costs. Senior citizens, longer trips, adventure activities, and higher coverage limits all increase the premium. Annual multi-trip policies offer better value for frequent travellers.

Purchase travel insurance as soon as you book your trip, ideally at least 7 to 15 days before departure. Early purchase ensures coverage for pre-departure cancellations and any unforeseen events — illness, natural disasters, or visa denial — that may occur before your journey begins. Buying early also maximises the coverage period of your policy.

Most standard travel insurance policies exclude pre-existing medical conditions. However, some Indian insurers offer policies with pre-existing condition coverage — typically for senior citizens or travellers with stable chronic conditions — subject to full disclosure, medical underwriting, and additional premium. You must disclose all pre-existing conditions at the time of purchase; failure to do so can result in claim rejection. Always read the policy wording regarding pre-existing condition definitions and waiting periods.

Most travel insurance policies must be purchased before departure. Some insurers allow purchase after departure but impose a waiting period (typically 24 to 72 hours) before coverage begins, and may exclude claims arising from events that occurred or were known before purchase. Post-departure policies generally do not cover pre-departure cancellation or events that were foreseeable at the time of purchase. The best practice is to buy before your trip starts.

The claim process typically involves five steps: (1) Notify your insurer immediately — most policies require notification within 24 to 48 hours of the event. (2) Collect all supporting documents — medical reports, police reports (for theft), airline delay certificates, original receipts, and claim forms. (3) Submit the claim with complete documentation to the insurer's claims department. (4) Track your claim status through the insurer's portal or customer service. (5) Receive reimbursement once the claim is approved. Insurers are required to settle or reject claims within 30 days of receiving all documents under IRDAI regulations. Visit our detailed guide on how to file a travel insurance claim for complete information.

Common exclusions across most travel insurance policies include: pre-existing medical conditions (unless specifically covered), high-risk adventure sports (unless an add-on is purchased), self-inflicted injuries, suicide or attempted suicide, claims arising from alcohol or drug use, travel to countries under government sanctions or travel advisories, war or civil unrest, non-emergency treatment, routine check-ups, and pregnancy-related claims beyond a specified week (typically 12 to 24 weeks). Loss or theft of unattended baggage, claims from illegal activities, and claims arising from not following travel advisories are also typically excluded.
